# Lead Link Constitutional Authority -- v5.0 Reference

This document provides the constitutional reference specific to the Lead Link role: its purpose, accountabilities, domain, and authority boundaries *as a Lead Link*. Load it for Lead Link-specific compliance questions, authority disputes, or when onboarding to the role.

**For the cross-role view** -- how Lead Link authority interacts with Facilitator, Secretary, and Rep Link authority, how the role-filler autonomy principle applies, and how to distinguish governance from operational decisions -- load `../shared/authority-boundaries.md` instead of or in addition to this file.

---

## The Lead Link Role Definition

**Purpose**: The Lead Link's purpose is identical to the circle's purpose. The Lead Link exists to ensure the circle can express that purpose through its organizational structure and the work performed within it.

**Accountabilities**:
1. Structuring the Governance of the Circle to enact its Purpose and Accountabilities
2. Assigning Partners to the Circle's Roles; monitoring the fit; offering feedback to enhance fit; and re-assigning Roles to other Partners when useful for enhancing fit
3. Allocating the Circle's resources across its various Projects and/or Roles
4. Establishing priorities and Strategies for the Circle
5. Defining metrics for the Circle (beyond those that role-fillers define for their own roles)
6. Removing constraints within the Super-Circle to the Circle

**Domain**: Role assignments within the Circle. The Lead Link is the sole authority for assigning Partners to Roles in the circle, absent a governance policy delegating this authority.

---

## What the Lead Link Can Do Unilaterally

These are Lead Link actions that do not require governance:

| Action | Constitutional Basis |
|--------|---------------------|
| Assign a Partner to a Role | Domain: Role assignments within the Circle |
| Remove a Partner from a Role | Domain: Role assignments within the Circle |
| Set or change the Circle's strategy | Accountability: Establishing priorities and Strategies |
| Set prioritization guidance for the circle | Accountability: Establishing priorities and Strategies |
| Decide how to allocate the circle's resources | Accountability: Allocating Circle resources |
| Define circle-level metrics | Accountability: Defining metrics for the Circle |
| Escalate constraints to the Super-Circle | Accountability: Removing constraints within the Super-Circle |
| Fill a role temporarily until someone else is assigned | Implicit in Lead Link assignment authority |

---

## What the Lead Link Cannot Do (Requires Governance)

These actions require governance meeting process, even if the Lead Link wants them:

| Action | Why Governance Is Required |
|--------|---------------------------|
| Create a new role | Role creation is governance -- modifies circle structure |
| Add or remove an accountability from an existing role | Accountability modification is governance |
| Claim a domain for a role | Domain allocation is governance |
| Create or modify a policy | Policy is governance |
| Convert a role into a sub-circle | Structural transformation is governance |
| Assign the Lead Link role itself | The Lead Link of a sub-circle is filled by the Super-Circle Lead Link (not elected) -- but the Super-Circle Lead Link makes this assignment, not the sub-circle Lead Link |
| Override a role-filler's authority within their domain | Domain authority belongs to the role, not the Lead Link |
| Direct how a role-filler fulfills their accountabilities | Role-fillers have autonomy within their role's authority |

## The Lead Link's Relationship to Role-Filler Autonomy

This is one of the most frequently misunderstood aspects of the Lead Link role, and the most important to get right.

**The Lead Link assigns people to roles. The role-filler then has full authority to fulfill those accountabilities as they see fit.**

This means:
- The Lead Link cannot instruct a role-filler on *how* to do their work, only *what* work is structurally defined
- The Lead Link cannot override a role-filler's decision within their accountabilities
- The Lead Link's authority is organizational (who fills the role) and strategic (what the circle prioritizes), not operational (how work gets done)

**The correct Lead Link response to a role-filler doing their job in a way the Lead Link disagrees with**:
- If the role definition is the problem -> bring a governance proposal to modify it
- If the fit is the problem -> have a fit conversation and potentially re-assign
- If the strategy is the problem -> clarify or adjust the strategy and communicate it

**What the Lead Link may not do**: Direct the role-filler's day-to-day work, veto decisions made within the role's authority, or treat the circle like a traditional reporting hierarchy.

## The Lead Link Is Not a Manager

In traditional organizations, a "Lead Link" sounds like a manager. The constitutional reality is narrower and more specific.

| Traditional Manager | Holacracy Lead Link |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| Directs how work is done | Assigns who does which work |
| Reviews and approves decisions | Does not approve role-filler decisions |
| Sets individual objectives | Sets circle strategy and priorities |
| Has authority over people | Has authority over role *assignments* |
| Can override subordinates | Cannot override role-filler authority |
| Manages performance | Manages fit-to-role |

The Lead Link can influence work through strategy and assignment. That is the full scope of the authority. If the Lead Link wants more influence over *how* work is done, the answer is governance -- add accountabilities that structure the work at the role level, then assign the right person to that role.

## Lead Link Selection

The Lead Link of a circle is *not* elected by the circle. The Lead Link is either:
- **Top-level circle**: Assigned by the process used to create or govern the overall organization (often the founding structure)
- **Sub-circle**: Assigned by the Super-Circle's Lead Link

This distinguishes the Lead Link from the Facilitator, Secretary, and Rep Link -- all of which are elected by the Integrative Election process. The Lead Link's organizational authority comes from above; the other Core Roles' authority comes from the circle itself.

**In a solo-operator context**: This distinction is less salient but conceptually important. The "assignment" of the Lead Link to the top-level circle is the founding structure of the organization itself.

## The Lead Link and the Super-Circle

The Lead Link sits at the boundary between the circle and the Super-Circle. This creates two important dynamics:

**1. Receiving constraints and guidance from above**

The Super-Circle's Lead Link communicates strategy, priorities, and resource constraints that affect this circle. The sub-circle Lead Link's job is to translate those constraints into how the sub-circle is structured and prioritized.

**2. Escalating constraints upward**

When the Super-Circle is creating constraints on the sub-circle's ability to express its purpose, the Lead Link is accountable for surfacing and attempting to remove those constraints. This is a formal accountability -- the Lead Link does not just absorb organizational obstacles, they are responsible for naming them to the Super-Circle.

## Authority Boundaries in Practice

**Scenario: A role-filler is not fulfilling a key accountability.**

The Lead Link's options:
1. Have a fit conversation -- "Is there something preventing you from fulfilling this accountability? How can I help?"
2. Re-assign if fit has deteriorated -- Lead Link authority
3. Bring a governance proposal if the accountability is unclear or needs restructuring -- governance process

The Lead Link does NOT: Override the role-filler's judgment on how to do the work, directly perform the work unilaterally as "Lead Link," or treat non-fulfillment as a management performance issue without first checking whether the problem is structural.

**Scenario: The Lead Link wants to restrict what a role can do.**

The Lead Link's options:
1. Modify the role's accountability through governance -- governance process required
2. Create a policy restricting the role's authority -- governance process required
3. Re-assign to someone who would exercise the authority more narrowly -- Lead Link authority

The Lead Link does NOT: Verbally instruct a role-filler to not exercise an accountability that is in their role definition. That is a governance change that requires governance process.

**Scenario: The Lead Link wants to set strategy but no one agrees.**

Strategy is the Lead Link's authority to set unilaterally. The Lead Link does not need consensus to set strategy. However:
- Good practice is to involve circle members in the sense-making that informs strategy
- The Lead Link must communicate strategy clearly and consistently -- people cannot align to unstated strategy
- If circle members believe the strategy is wrong, they can bring a governance proposal to add an accountability to the Lead Link role requiring a collaborative strategy process
- The Lead Link can update strategy as often as needed -- it is not locked in governance
