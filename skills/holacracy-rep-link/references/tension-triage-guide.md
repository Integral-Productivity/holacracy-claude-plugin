# Tension Triage Guide for Rep Links

> **Cross-reference.** The role-vs-person Step 1 decision tree and the meeting-type routing logic (governance vs. tactical) are the canonical references for *all* tension capture in this plugin -- not just Rep Link work. They live at [`../../shared/tension-triage.md`](../../shared/tension-triage.md). The Rep Link role layers an additional decision (does the tension cross the circle boundary into the enclosing circle?) on top of those universal gates, which is what this guide covers. If you are evaluating a general tension, start with the shared guide; come here when the question is specifically about whether to escalate.

Extended guidance on the most nuanced Rep Link accountability: determining which tensions cross the circle boundary.

---

## Why Triage Matters

The Rep Link is a signal in the governance system. When the signal is too noisy -- carrying local tensions, personal frustrations, or issues that the sub-circle can resolve itself -- the enclosing circle starts to filter it out. When the signal is too quiet -- when the Rep Link only carries obvious structural issues -- the sub-circle's experience of the broader organization becomes invisible to enclosing circle governance.

Good triage is not conservatism. A Rep Link who almost never carries tensions is not doing the role well. The sub-circle's operational reality should be flowing upward regularly. The question is always: *is this tension one the enclosing circle's governance or coordination can actually address?*

---

## The Triage Decision Tree

Use this in sequence. Stop at the first answer that resolves the question.

### Step 1 -- Is this a role tension or a person tension?

The Rep Link carries role tensions. A role tension is a gap between what a role is currently able to do and what it could do -- a structural or operational gap that governance can address. A person tension is about how an individual is showing up in their role -- their behavior, reliability, communication, or performance.

**Role tension example:** "The Technology Architecture role has no clear accountability for approving vendor integrations, so when Platform roles ask for approval, there's no one to ask."

**Person tension example:** "The person filling the Lead Link role isn't following through on things they agree to in tactical meetings."

If it's a person tension: the Integrative Decision Record (IDR) process exists for this. Route it there. The Rep Link should not carry person tensions to the enclosing circle's governance -- the enclosing circle cannot and should not govern individual behavior through role structure.

Edge case: *Sometimes what presents as a person tension is actually a role tension in disguise.* If the same behavior keeps recurring across different role-fillers, or if the problem is that there's no governance structure holding the person accountable, that may be a structural tension. Ask: "If a different person energized this role tomorrow, would the same problem exist?" If yes, it's structural.

---

### Step 2 -- Can it be resolved within the sub-circle?

The sub-circle has its own Lead Link, its own governance, and its own tactical process. Many tensions that feel like they need external help can actually be resolved locally.

**What the sub-circle can do without the enclosing circle:**
- Create, modify, or remove roles within the sub-circle (through the sub-circle's governance process)
- Create policies governing how its roles operate
- Establish its own strategy within any strategy the enclosing circle has set
- Set priorities and allocate its own resources among its roles
- Process tensions about how the sub-circle's work is organized internally

**Triage test:** Ask -- "Could the sub-circle's Lead Link, using their legitimate authority, resolve this tension?" If yes, bring it to the sub-circle's governance or tactical meeting. *Keep local.*

Common error: treating the Lead Link as the problem when the Lead Link is actually the solution. If the Lead Link *could* resolve this but hasn't, the first step is raising it with the Lead Link in the sub-circle's tactical meeting -- not escalating to the enclosing circle.

---

### Step 3 -- Does it require something only the enclosing circle can provide?

Some things genuinely can only be changed by the enclosing circle. These are the legitimate Rep Link tensions:

**Resource allocation from the enclosing circle:** If the sub-circle needs more of a resource -- budget, tooling, headcount, physical space -- that the enclosing Lead Link controls, this is a tactical agenda item for the enclosing circle. It's not a governance issue (governance doesn't allocate resources), but it belongs in the enclosing circle's tactical meeting.

**Structural change to the enclosing circle's governance:** If the sub-circle needs a new role in the enclosing circle, a modified accountability, a new policy, or a domain clarification -- and these changes affect the enclosing circle's structure, not just the sub-circle's -- this is an enclosing circle governance tension.

**Cross-sub-circle coordination:** If another sub-circle of the enclosing circle is causing a recurring blockage and direct coordination between the sub-circles has not resolved it, the Rep Link can surface this as a tension in the enclosing circle's tactical meeting. The enclosing Lead Link can then facilitate coordination or bring a governance proposal to clarify accountabilities.

**Removing a policy or domain constraint:** If an enclosing circle policy or domain claim is limiting the sub-circle's ability to energize its work -- and the sub-circle has no legitimate path to modify it internally -- this belongs in the enclosing circle's governance.

---

### Step 4 -- Has it already been tried locally?

Escalation is warranted when local resolution has been genuinely attempted. If the tension has never been raised in the sub-circle's tactical or governance meeting, it's usually premature to carry it outward.

Exception: if the tension is clearly structural (Step 3) and there is no plausible local path, raising it in the sub-circle first is not necessary -- but it can still be useful for getting clarity and ensuring the tension is properly formed before bringing it to the enclosing circle.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1 -- Keep local

**Tension:** "The Marketing role in our sub-circle keeps doing work that overlaps with the Content role's accountabilities. There's confusion about who owns what."

**Triage:**
- Role or person tension? Role tension -- it's about unclear accountability scope.
- Can it be resolved within the sub-circle? Yes -- the sub-circle's governance meeting can clarify the roles' accountabilities, remove duplications, or create a policy.
- **Decision: Keep local. Bring to the sub-circle's governance meeting.**

---

### Example 2 -- Tactical meeting (enclosing circle)

**Tension:** "We're trying to deploy a new tool, but we need approval from the IT Governance role in the enclosing circle, and we've been waiting three weeks with no response."

**Triage:**
- Role or person tension? Primarily a coordination issue -- the IT Governance role exists and has the accountability, but isn't responding.
- Can it be resolved within the sub-circle? No -- the enclosing circle role is the constraint.
- Does it require structural change? Not necessarily -- the accountability exists. The problem is coordination, not structure.
- **Decision: Tactical agenda item in the enclosing circle. Rep Link raises: "I need the IT Governance role to respond to our approval request for [tool] so we can proceed with [project]."**

If the problem persists after multiple tactical meetings, *then* it may become a governance tension about whether the IT Governance role's accountability needs to be made more specific, or whether a policy should require response timelines.

---

### Example 3 -- Governance meeting (enclosing circle)

**Tension:** "Our sub-circle is accountable for managing the organization's vendor relationships, but we keep running into situations where the Finance role in the enclosing circle also acts on vendor contracts -- sometimes without coordinating with us. There's no clear governance line."

**Triage:**
- Role or person tension? Role tension -- it's a governance gap about accountability overlap between circles.
- Can it be resolved within the sub-circle? No -- the enclosing circle's Finance role is involved.
- Does it require structural change to the enclosing circle's governance? Yes -- either the Finance role's accountability needs to be scoped more precisely, or a policy needs to clarify who acts on what.
- **Decision: Governance meeting tension in the enclosing circle. The Rep Link brings this as a tension and may propose either a policy or an accountability modification.**

---

### Example 4 -- IDR, not a Rep Link tension

**Tension:** "The person filling the Lead Link role of our sub-circle is making decisions without consulting anyone, which is creating conflict and people are disengaged."

**Triage:**
- Role or person tension? Person tension -- the behavior of an individual in a role.
- Even if structural? Consider: "If a different person filled the Lead Link role tomorrow, would the problem disappear?" If yes -- person tension.
- **Decision: IDR process, or a direct relationship conversation facilitated appropriately. Not a Rep Link tension.**

Note: if the *outcome* of this behavior is creating structural problems (e.g., the Lead Link is making decisions outside their governance authority), the *structural impact* may be a governance tension -- but it should be framed as the governance gap, not the person's behavior.

---

### Example 5 -- Borderline: role tension in disguise

**Tension:** "For the third time this quarter, someone has made a major product decision that affected our sub-circle's work without any coordination. It's different people each time."

**Triage:**
- Role or person tension? On the surface -- multiple different people, so probably structural.
- "If a different person filled these roles, would the same problem exist?" Likely yes -- there's no accountability for cross-circle coordination on product decisions.
- Does it require enclosing circle structural change? Possibly -- if no role in the enclosing circle owns cross-circle product coordination, that's a governance gap.
- **Decision: Governance meeting tension. The Rep Link surfaces the pattern (not any individual's behavior) as evidence of a structural gap and proposes that the enclosing circle create or clarify an accountability for cross-circle product decision coordination.**

## Common Rep Link Triage Mistakes

**Carrying "the sub-circle is unhappy" to the enclosing circle.** This is advocacy, not tension-carrying. The Rep Link surfaces specific, experienced gaps -- not sentiment or collective preference. Reframe: what specific work is being limited or made harder, and by what governance condition?

**Waiting for an obvious crisis before bringing tensions.** Early, smaller tensions are easier to process. A Rep Link who only escalates after things have broken down has let the governance system fail to do its job.

**Over-filtering: almost never having anything to bring.** If the Rep Link never has tensions to surface, something is off. Either the sub-circle is unusually well-served by the enclosing circle's governance (possible), the Rep Link isn't listening to sub-circle members (common), or the Rep Link has internalized the Lead Link's perspective so thoroughly that they've lost the distinct viewpoint the role exists to provide (also common, especially if the same person has filled both roles in the past).

**Carrying tensions that aren't yours to carry.** The Rep Link carries tensions they have *experienced in the role* -- or that they have received from other sub-circle Circle Members who can't bring them directly. They don't carry tensions on behalf of people who haven't asked, or tensions from outside the sub-circle.

**Conflating "I disagree with the enclosing Lead Link's decision" with a governance tension.** Lead Links have broad authority within their circles. If the tension is "I wish the Lead Link had decided differently," that is not a governance tension -- unless the Lead Link acted outside their governance authority, in which case the tension is about the structural boundary, not the decision itself.
