# Rep Link Constitutional Duties Reference

Source: Holacracy Constitution v5.0

---

## Rep Link Role Definition (verbatim from Constitution S.2.6.4)

**Purpose:**
Within the Sub-Circle: Tensions relevant to process in the broader Circle channeled out and resolved.

**Accountabilities:**
- Seeking to understand Tensions conveyed by Circle Members of the Sub-Circle, and discerning those appropriate to process in the broader Circle
- Providing visibility to the Lead Link of the broader Circle into the health of the Sub-Circle, including reporting on any Metrics or Checklist items assigned to the whole Sub-Circle
- Removing constraints within the broader Circle that limit the Sub-Circle

**Note on election:** The Rep Link is elected by the Sub-Circle's Circle Members using the Holacracy integrative election process, not appointed by a Lead Link. This distinction matters: the role exists to represent the sub-circle's experience of the governance system, not the Lead Link's intent.

---

## Rep Link vs. Lead Link -- The Critical Distinction

These two roles link a sub-circle to an enclosing circle in opposite directions. Confusion between them is common and consequential.

| | Lead Link | Rep Link |
|---|---|---|
| **Direction** | Enclosing -> Sub-circle (links INTO the sub-circle) | Sub-circle -> Enclosing (links OUT of the sub-circle) |
| **Accountability source** | Appointed by enclosing circle Lead Link | Elected by sub-circle Circle Members |
| **What they carry** | Enclosing circle's needs, resources, and strategy into the sub-circle | Sub-circle's tensions and health indicators into the enclosing circle |
| **Authority** | Sets sub-circle strategy, assigns roles, allocates resources | Carries tensions; participates as full enclosing Circle Member |
| **Advocacy?** | Yes -- for the broader organization's priorities | No -- for *tensions*, not positions or preferences |

The Rep Link has no authority to negotiate, commit to, or speak for the sub-circle's decisions. What it can do is ensure that tensions experienced in the sub-circle -- gaps between current reality and what is possible -- reach the governance system that can process them.

---

## Full Constitutional Membership in the Enclosing Circle

The Rep Link is a full Circle Member of the enclosing circle, with all the rights and obligations that entails (Constitution S.2.4):

- Can add agenda items to tactical meetings
- Can raise tensions in governance meetings and submit proposals
- Participates in all agenda items, not just sub-circle reporting
- Can object to governance proposals that create a valid objection
- Can be elected to roles within the enclosing circle (except Lead Link, which is a link role itself)

This is important to internalize: the Rep Link does not attend enclosing circle meetings to report and leave. They are a member, and their perspective -- grounded in the sub-circle's operational reality -- is a legitimate and valuable contribution to the enclosing circle's governance.

## What the Rep Link Reports in Tactical Meetings

Constitution S.3.3.2 specifies tactical meeting structure. The Rep Link's reporting obligations in an enclosing circle tactical meeting:

1. **Checklist items assigned to the sub-circle** -- any recurring actions the sub-circle as a whole is accountable for in the enclosing circle's view. Report yes/no since last meeting.

2. **Metrics assigned to the sub-circle** -- any indicators the sub-circle tracks on behalf of the enclosing circle. Report current value.

3. **Project updates** -- any projects the sub-circle owns that the enclosing circle tracks. Brief status update: what happened since the last meeting.

4. **Tensions as agenda items** -- any tensions from the sub-circle that belong in the enclosing circle's tactical space. Added to the agenda in the standard way (label only; no discussion during agenda building).

The Rep Link is not obligated to report on everything happening in the sub-circle -- only what is materially relevant to the enclosing circle's governance and coordination.

## Governance Proposal Format

When carrying a structural tension to a governance meeting, proposals take this form:

```
[Role Name or "Anchor Circle"] is hereby modified as follows:

[TYPE OF CHANGE]
[Specific text of the change]
```

**Common proposal types:**

**Adding an accountability to an existing role:**
```
[Role Name] is hereby modified as follows:

Added accountability:
[Exact accountability text -- verb + object, no qualifiers]
```

**Creating a new role:**
```
A new Role is hereby created within [Circle Name]:

Name: [Role Name]
Purpose: [Purpose statement -- what the role exists to achieve]
Accountabilities:
- [Accountability 1]
- [Accountability 2]
Domain (if needed): [What this role exclusively controls]
```

**Modifying or removing a policy:**
```
The following Policy of [Circle Name] is hereby [modified / removed]:

[Original policy name]

[If modified, new text:]
[New policy text -- precise and complete]
```

**Adding a domain to a role (or revoking/clarifying scope):**
```
[Role Name] is hereby modified as follows:

Added Domain: [What this role now exclusively controls]
```

**Important constraints on proposals:**
- A proposal must address a tension *you have actually experienced* in your role -- you cannot propose on behalf of someone else's tension without their involvement
- A proposal cannot address multiple unrelated tensions -- one tension, one proposal
- A proposal cannot prescribe a solution beyond what is needed to resolve the tension
- If the Facilitator determines the proposal cannot possibly cause harm even if it creates other tensions, it may pass without full objection testing (Constitution S.5.4.3)

## Valid Objections -- What the Rep Link Needs to Know

When the Rep Link brings a proposal, they may receive objections. Understanding valid objections helps prepare better proposals and anticipate genuine concerns.

An objection is valid if and only if it meets all four criteria (Constitution S.5.5.1):

1. **Degrades capacity** -- The proposal would reduce the circle's or objector's capacity to express its Purpose or enact its Accountabilities
2. **Caused by the proposal** -- The degradation is created by the proposal, not by circumstances that already exist
3. **Not yet existing** -- It's not already a known tension being processed elsewhere
4. **Real or imminent** -- Based on known dynamics, not speculation about what might happen

The Facilitator tests each objection against these criteria. The Rep Link can help the proposer anticipate and address objections by reviewing the proposal against these four tests before the meeting.

**Common objections to Rep Link proposals:**
- "This accountability is already covered by [another role]" -- check governance for overlaps before proposing
- "This domain is too broad" -- narrow domain claims to the minimum necessary
- "This removes flexibility the Lead Link needs" -- ensure proposals don't inadvertently constrain the Lead Link's governance authority
- "This is a person issue, not a structural issue" -- the strongest signal that a tension may not be a governance matter

## The Rep Link's Role in Sub-circle Governance

The Rep Link is a Circle Member of the sub-circle as well, elected by those Circle Members. This means the Rep Link participates in the sub-circle's own governance and tactical meetings.

Within the sub-circle, the Rep Link:
- Can raise tensions that affect its role (carrying tensions upward is itself an accountability)
- Can request that the sub-circle process tensions before deciding whether they need escalation
- Does **not** have special authority within the sub-circle beyond being a Circle Member
- Can provide feedback from the enclosing circle back into the sub-circle (for example: "The enclosing circle's Lead Link has shared that X is a priority for us -- that might be a tension worth processing in governance")
