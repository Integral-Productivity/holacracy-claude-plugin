# Holacracy Authority Boundaries -- Shared Reference

This document is shared across all holacracy skills. It provides the definitive cross-role reference for authority boundaries: what each Core Role can decide unilaterally, what requires governance, how roles defer to each other, and how the role-filler autonomy principle operates in practice.

Load this when authority questions arise that span more than one Core Role, when a practitioner is confused about which role has standing to act, or when navigating a boundary dispute between process, records, organizational, and role-level authority.

---

## The Four Authority Types in Holacracy

Each Core Role holds a distinct, non-overlapping authority type. The system works because these types are complementary rather than hierarchical.

| Core Role | Authority Type | Scope | What It Cannot Do |
|-----------|---------------|-------|-------------------|
| **Facilitator** | Process | Within meetings: the format, sequence, and validity of what happens | Cannot decide outcomes; cannot override role-filler authority outside meetings; cannot change governance |
| **Secretary** | Records and Interpretation | Outside meetings: the official governance record and constitutional rulings | Cannot facilitate a meeting; cannot assign people to roles; cannot set strategy |
| **Lead Link** | Organizational | Continuously: role assignments, strategy, priorities, resource allocation | Cannot direct how role-fillers fulfill their accountabilities; cannot change governance unilaterally |
| **Rep Link** | Representation | Upward: carries circle tensions to the Super-Circle's governance | Cannot create governance in the circle; cannot assign roles; has no internal authority over the circle |

**The key insight**: No Core Role has authority over what another Core Role owns in its domain. Conflicts between Core Roles are resolved by this typology, not by seniority.

## The Role-Filler Autonomy Principle

This is the most important and most frequently misunderstood rule in Holacracy. It applies universally and constrains every Core Role including the Lead Link.

> **A role-filler has full authority to fulfill their accountabilities as they see fit, within the governance boundaries defined for their role.**

This means:
- No one -- including the Lead Link -- can tell a role-filler *how* to do their work within their accountability
- The Lead Link can define *what* work belongs in a role (through governance) and *who* fills the role (through assignment) -- but not the operational execution
- The Facilitator cannot direct tactical decisions
- The Secretary cannot prescribe how governance proposals are written, only whether the process was followed

**The correct response to "I don't like how this person is doing their role":**

| Source of the problem | Correct response | Incorrect response |
|-----------------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Role definition is unclear | Governance proposal to clarify accountabilities | Verbally instructing the role-filler |
| Wrong person in the role | Lead Link re-assigns | Directing how the current role-filler works |
| Role-filler is not fulfilling accountabilities | Fit conversation -> re-assignment or governance | Overriding their decisions directly |
| The accountability structure is wrong | Governance proposal to restructure | Workaround via informal direction |

## What Requires Governance vs. What Is Operational

This is the second most common source of confusion in Holacracy practice. The line is clean once understood.

### Requires Governance (Governance Meeting Process)

These changes can only be made through the Integrative Decision-Making process in a Governance meeting:

- Creating a new Role (name, purpose, accountabilities, domains)
- Modifying an existing Role's purpose, accountabilities, or domains
- Removing a Role
- Creating, modifying, or removing a Policy
- Defining or removing a Domain held by a Role or Circle
- Delegating a Domain from the Circle to a Role (or rescinding it)
- Adopting or changing Circle Strategy (when done through governance, not Lead Link authority)
- Converting a Role into a Sub-Circle (or dissolving a Sub-Circle into roles)
- Electing Core Roles: Facilitator, Secretary, Rep Link
- Releasing a Core Role election early

### Operational (No Governance Required)

These can be decided by the relevant role-filler or Core Role without meeting:

- Lead Link: assigning or un-assigning a Partner to/from a Role
- Lead Link: setting or changing Circle Strategy and priorities
- Lead Link: allocating resources across Projects and Roles
- Lead Link: defining metrics for the Circle
- Lead Link: filling a Role temporarily pending a permanent assignment
- Any role-filler: how they fulfill their own accountabilities
- Any role-filler: creating, updating, or closing their own Projects
- Any role-filler: requesting next actions from other roles in tactical meetings
- Secretary: scheduling meetings, maintaining records, issuing constitutional rulings
- Facilitator: ruling on process questions during meetings

### The Grey Zone

When it is unclear which side the line falls on, ask: "Does this change the *structure* of authority, or does it change how someone *exercises* existing authority?"

- Changing structure (who owns what accountability, what a role is allowed to do) -> Governance
- Changing exercise (how someone uses existing authority, what projects they take on) -> Operational

## Core Role Interactions and Deference

### Facilitator and Lead Link

The Facilitator does not report to the Lead Link. Their authority is constitutional and independent.
- The Lead Link cannot direct the Facilitator on how to run meetings
- The Lead Link cannot override the Facilitator's process rulings during a meeting
- The Facilitator cannot make organizational decisions (assignments, strategy, allocation)
- If the Lead Link disagrees with how the Facilitator is running meetings, the correct response is a fit conversation or a governance proposal -- not a directive

**Where they cooperate**: The Lead Link sets the strategic context; the Facilitator ensures the meeting process is constitutionally valid so that context can be operationalized. They are partners with distinct domains.

### Secretary and Lead Link

The Secretary holds the governance record; the Lead Link holds organizational authority.
- The Secretary does not need Lead Link approval to issue a constitutional ruling
- The Lead Link does not need Secretary approval to assign a Partner to a Role
- When the Lead Link wants to change governance, they bring a proposal -- the Secretary captures the outcome
- When the Secretary interprets governance, the Lead Link must respect the ruling (even if they disagree -- the correct response is an appeal to the Super-Circle Secretary, not a directive to the Secretary)

**Where they cooperate**: The Secretary ensures governance records are accurate so the Lead Link is working from correct information about what roles and accountabilities actually exist.

### Secretary and Facilitator

The Secretary and Facilitator are the process partners of every meeting.
- **During meetings**: The Facilitator owns process; the Secretary owns capture. The Secretary does not interject on process questions.
- **Outside meetings**: The Secretary owns constitutional interpretation; the Facilitator has no interpretive authority outside a meeting context.
- If they disagree on a constitutional question outside of a meeting, the Secretary's interpretation governs. A party who disagrees can appeal to the Super-Circle Secretary.

**Where they cooperate**: The Secretary's pre-meeting prep (pulling GlassFrog data) feeds the Facilitator's meeting agenda. The Secretary's post-meeting record reflects what the Facilitator's process produced.

### Rep Link and Lead Link

The Rep Link and Lead Link are organizational counterweights.
- The **Lead Link** carries authority and constraints *down* from the Super-Circle into the circle
- The **Rep Link** carries tensions and perspective *up* from the circle into the Super-Circle
- Neither has authority over the other's direction of travel
- The Lead Link cannot direct the Rep Link on what tensions to carry upward -- the Rep Link uses their own judgment about what is relevant to the Super-Circle

**Where they differ from a traditional hierarchy**: In a management hierarchy, information flows through the same authority channel in both directions. In Holacracy, the inward and outward flows are separated by design -- the Lead Link cannot filter or veto what the Rep Link surfaces upward.

## Domain Authority: How It Works

A Domain is exclusive authority over something. When a Role holds a Domain, only that role can authorize others to impact it.

**How domain authority operates in practice:**

1. If Role A holds a Domain over [X], no other role can modify [X] without Role A's permission
2. Role A can grant standing permission by requesting a Policy through governance
3. Role A can grant case-by-case permission directly (does not require governance)
4. Without permission, impact on the Domain is a constitutional violation -- regardless of intent or seniority

**The Lead Link's assignment Domain**: The Lead Link holds a Domain over role assignments within the circle. This means:
- No other role can assign or un-assign Partners without the Lead Link's authorization
- A governance policy can create standing permission for others to make assignments
- Absent such a policy, all assignment actions go through the Lead Link

**What Domains are not**: Domains do not give a role authority over everything *related to* that thing. A role with a Domain over the company website has authority over the website itself -- it does not have authority to direct other roles on what content to create for the website. Content creation belongs to the accountabilities of the roles that own it; the website domain governs who can publish it.

## Authority During Meetings vs. Outside Meetings

Meeting context shifts who holds what authority:

| Situation | Who has authority | Authority type |
|-----------|------------------|---------------|
| During a Tactical or Governance meeting | Facilitator | Process rulings -- overrides anyone departing from format |
| During a meeting, on governance questions | Facilitator | Validity of objections, tensions, proposals |
| Outside meetings, on constitutional questions | Secretary | Interpretive rulings on the Constitution |
| Outside meetings, on assignments and strategy | Lead Link | Organizational decisions |
| During an IDM process, on whether to adopt | The circle's IDM outcome | Constitutional process |
| On role-filler work within their accountability | The role-filler | Autonomous within governance boundaries |

**Edge case**: If the Facilitator makes a process ruling in a meeting that the Secretary believes is constitutionally incorrect, the Secretary should not override it mid-meeting (that would undermine the process). The correct response is to raise a process challenge through the Facilitator's own process (ask the Facilitator to reconsider) or address it post-meeting through a constitutional ruling or Super-Circle appeal.

## Common Authority Boundary Violations (Anti-Patterns)

These are the patterns that most frequently create organizational friction in Holacracy practice:

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Violates Boundaries | Correct Response |
|-------------|---------------------------|-----------------|
| Lead Link directing how a role fulfills its accountabilities | Violates role-filler autonomy | Change the role definition through governance if the accountability needs to change |
| Facilitator being directed by the Lead Link on how to run meetings | Violates Facilitator's process authority | Lead Link brings a governance proposal if the meeting structure is the problem |
| Secretary being told by Lead Link not to issue a constitutional ruling | Violates Secretary's interpretive authority | Secretary proceeds; Lead Link can appeal to Super-Circle Secretary |
| Role-filler acting on someone else's Domain without permission | Violates Domain authority | Request permission or a standing policy through governance |
| Lead Link un-assigning someone from a role without conversation | Constitutionally valid but organizationally damaging | Lead Link should communicate the change directly before executing it |
| Using assignment authority to "punish" poor performance | Misuses organizational authority as a management tool | Address performance through direct conversation; re-assign only when it genuinely serves the circle |
| Governance changes decided in tactical meetings | Tactical meetings have no governance authority | Defer to a governance meeting; note the tension in the tactical record |
| Facilitator declaring an objection valid to avoid conflict | Violates process integrity | Test the objection constitutionally; invalid objections must be named as invalid |
